Does Stress Predispose Your Body to Injury?

Luka Doncic and The Mind-Body Connection

Thursday, April 16, 2026 6 min read
Table of contents

  • The Stressors

The Injury

I recently saw this guy on Instagram Reels, Stefan Duvivier or @airduvi. I think he’s a physiotherapist or a trainer or something like that. He was talking about Luka Doncic, the professional basketball player for the LA Lakers, who recently suffered a season-ending hamstring injury during a game (grade 2 strain, or partial muscle tear). Devastating news on its own for any high level athlete and their fans.

The hot take? That Luka’s injury had much less to do with how many minutes he was playing on the court, and much more to do with the amount of stress he was experiencing off the court.

Luka is currently one of the most valuable athletes in the entire NBA, having recently signed a $165 million dollar contract for 3 years. He has access to some of the very best doctors, physios, athletic trainers and coaches from around the world, not to mention equipment and treatments and facilities. He has been in prime physical condition, losing more than 20 lbs last summer. However, despite all that going for him, his body was still breaking down, and repeatedly. All at just 27 years old, at a time when LeBron, who is 14 years his senior, is still thriving in his 22nd season. So what gives?

Maybe it’s just plain old bad luck. Sometimes the stars don’t align. Or maybe it has something to do with Luka’s whole world flipping upside down in the past year.

The Stressors

He was traded from the Dallas Mavericks back in February 2025 in a trade he didn’t see coming. His partner of 10 years and fiancée leaves in May back to Slovenia and takes their daughter with her. His second daughter is born in December, and apparently the police were called during a disagreement at the hospital. He then returned to the US on his own and since then has called off the engagement and is now embroiled in a bitter and very public custody battle over the two kids, who he hasn’t even seen since December.

Maybe his nervous system was activated in ‘fight or flight mode’. As a result, maybe his deep hip flexors were chronically tense and tilted his pelvis forward, lengthening his hamstrings and putting them at a higher risk of injury at baseline. Maybe they’ve been building up tension with every step he took, until a big enough disruption finally overwhelms the ability of the muscle fibres to keep it all together.

“Your hamstring doesn’t just randomly tear. It tears because the tissue has been under seige for months from a nervous system that never got to calm down.”

― Stefan Duvivier

The Research

Stefan cited a systematic reviaw titled “Psychosocial Factors and Sport Injuries: Meta-analyses for Prediction and Prevention”, which was published by Swedish researchers in the peer-reviewed journal Sports Medicine in 2016. They looked at 48 published studies that looked at psychosocial factors and injury rates, and found that the predictor with the strongest association with injuries rates was stress response (r=0.27) and history of stressors (r=0.13), while coping and personality traits had weaker associations (r=-0.07, and 0.01 respectively). They also found that psychosocial interventions studied (such as stress management), was associated with fewer injuries (r=-0.31).

In terms of why this might be, the authors proposed that exposure to prolonged stress could lead to actual brain tissues changes, specifically decreased communication between the left and right cerebral hemispheres, and result in decreased decision-making ability, decision-making speed, and attentional capacity, which are all associated with increased injury risk.

a consensus statement article from the peer-reviewed journal Sports Medicine published in June 2024 titled “50 Years of Research on the Psychology of Sport Injury”. It looked at meta-analyses (studies of studies) which found that stress responses (more precisely, how athletes respond to life stressors rather than the stressors themselves) had the strongest association with injury rates, with an effect size of r=0.27.

The proposed (expert consensus) mechanisms for this association are threefold: attentional changes (distractability, mental fatigue), physiologic changes (increased muscle tension and decreased propioception), and cognitive impairment (reduced ability to process environmental cues and respond appropriately). In terms of interventions, teaching athletes stress management techniques have been shown to reduce injury rates over a season. Athletes who had protective factors, higher resiliency, optimism, self-efficacy, and social supports, tended to have lower injury risk.

Stefan argues that in chronic states of stress, cortisol is elevated, muscles are slower to recover, and the body has decreased awareness or propioception, and that no amount of physiotherapy or adjustments or exercises are going to resolve injuries until the underlying cause is addressed and dealt with.

“When the nervous system doesn’t feel safe, when the ground underneth your life is unstable, the [muscles] grip and try to create stability that the rest of the system up top can’t provide for you.”

― Stefan Duvivier

I’ve always been interested in stuff like this, including other topics like The Myth of Normal and trauma with Gabor Maté, but also the idea of the mind-gut axis and IBS. Also, things like complex regional pain syndrome or fibromyalgia, and the idea of total pain in palliative care. How stressors, existential suffering, anxiety, and mood can modulate the physical pain that we experience, which aren’t always fully able to be treated with pain meds and pharmacology alone

It made me think about my own chronic back pain. This all started as I tell myself in my brain and how I make sense of it. It all started in undergrad, first year of college. I was on the novice men’s rowing team and I was the weakest guy who barely made it on the team after some other guy got injured, apparently. From the beginning, I always felt an inferiority complex and felt a lot of pressure and stress to work hard and get stronger to literally pull my weight. We had early morning practices on the water starting at 5:00, and this coincided with starting to live away from home for the first time as well.

I was told by a coach that I needed to gain 20 or 30 lb, which I really tried hard to do but was really difficult for me at the time. Anyway, now I wish I could lose some more weight, haha. At that time, I was also starting the grind for med school and starting to code and joining a startup, feeling the pressure there to feel a bit out of my depth and then having to sort of compensate and hide any deficiencies. There was also probably a lot of conflict with my roommate at the time, disagreements and a sense of competition and competitiveness. I think all that, and also my parents being separated. I think my body was holding on to that. That was also the time when I was probably the most depressed and using the most porn and procrastinating by staying up late and eating late-night food and chasing after girls, not really having a sense of identity. Looking back, it was a turbulent time for me.
